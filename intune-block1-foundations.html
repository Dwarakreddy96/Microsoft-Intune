TOPIC 01
What is MDM and MAM?
Mobile Device Management vs Mobile Application Management
The Core Problem They Solve

Imagine you're an IT admin at a 500-person company. Your employees use laptops, phones, tablets — some company-owned, some personal. They access company email, SharePoint, Teams. What happens when someone loses their phone? What if an employee installs a malicious app? What if they leave the company with all the data on their personal phone?

MDM and MAM are the two solutions Microsoft (and the industry) came up with to handle this.

MDM — Mobile Device Management
📱
What It Is
MDM gives your IT team complete control over an enrolled device — the whole device, not just apps. You can push policies, wipe the device, see what's installed, enforce encryption.
🎯
Best Used For
Company-owned devices — laptops, phones, tablets. When the company owns the hardware, MDM is the right tool because you have legal and technical authority over the full device.
⚙️
What You Can Do
Wipe the entire device, enforce BitLocker, push Wi-Fi/VPN settings, control which apps can be installed, set password requirements, block USB ports.
🔑 Key Concept

When a device is MDM-enrolled, it gets a certificate and an MDM agent installed. This agent constantly checks in with Intune (every 8 hours by default, or when triggered) to receive and apply policies. Think of it like a remote control for the device.

MAM — Mobile Application Management

MAM works at the app level, not the device level. You don't need to enroll the entire device — you just wrap certain apps (like Outlook, Teams, OneDrive) with policies that protect corporate data inside those apps.

🔒
What It Controls
Copy/paste between apps, save-to-personal-storage, screenshot restrictions, PIN for app access, selective wipe of only corporate data in the app.
👤
BYOD Scenarios
Employees use personal phones. Company can't touch the personal phone — but CAN protect data inside the Outlook/Teams app. MAM makes this possible.
🗑️
Selective Wipe
When an employee leaves, IT does a selective wipe — only company data in those managed apps is removed. Personal photos, WhatsApp etc. are untouched.
MDM vs MAM — Side by Side
Factor MDM MAM
ScopeEntire deviceSpecific apps only
EnrollmentDevice must be enrolledNo device enrollment needed (MAM-WE)
Best forCompany-owned devicesBYOD (personal devices)
Wipe capabilityFull device wipeSelective wipe (apps only)
PrivacyLow — IT can see full deviceHigh — IT sees only app data
Control levelHighMedium
User experienceMore restrictedLess intrusive
MAM-WE — The Game Changer

MAM Without Enrollment (MAM-WE) is one of the most powerful concepts in Intune. It means you can apply app protection policies to personal devices WITHOUT enrolling them into MDM. The user just signs into the Outlook app with their company account, and your MAM policies automatically apply.

🏢
Real-World Scenario

Company: TCS India, 5,000 employees.
Problem: Employees want to check work email on personal phones. IT doesn't want to enroll personal phones (privacy concerns, employee resistance).
Solution: Deploy MAM-WE. Employees install Outlook from the app store, sign in with their TCS account. Intune applies MAM policies — they can't copy company email to WhatsApp, can't save attachments to their personal Google Drive, app requires PIN. When employee resigns, IT selectively wipes only the Outlook app data. The employee's personal photos, contacts — untouched.
Result: Company data protected. Employee privacy respected. Zero device enrollment needed.

When to Use Which (Decision Guide)
  • Company laptop/desktop: Always MDM. You own it, you control it.
  • Company-issued mobile: MDM (full control) + MAM (app-level DLP on top)
  • Employee personal phone (BYOD): MAM-WE only. Never force MDM on personal devices — legal and ethical issues.
  • Contractor/vendor device: MAM-WE — they have their own device management, just protect your apps.
  • Shared kiosk device: MDM with kiosk mode configuration.
🎤 Interview Questions — Topic 1
What is the difference between MDM and MAM?
MDM manages the entire device and requires enrollment. MAM manages only specific apps and their data. MDM is for company-owned devices; MAM is ideal for BYOD scenarios where we can't enroll the full device.
What is MAM without enrollment (MAM-WE) and when would you use it?
MAM-WE applies app protection policies to devices that are not enrolled in MDM. I would use it for BYOD scenarios — employees use personal phones to access company apps like Outlook, but IT only controls data within those apps without managing the whole device.
Can you use both MDM and MAM together?
Yes. For company-owned devices, you'd use MDM for device-level control AND add MAM policies on top for additional app-level data protection. This gives you the highest level of security.
What happens when you do a selective wipe vs a full wipe?
Full wipe (MDM) resets the entire device to factory settings — all data is gone. Selective wipe (MAM) only removes company data from managed apps — the user's personal data stays intact.
TOPIC 02
What is Microsoft Intune?
Purpose, History, Architecture & Licensing Overview
The Simple Definition

Microsoft Intune is a cloud-based endpoint management solution that lets IT administrators manage and secure devices, apps, and data across an organization — without needing any on-premises infrastructure.

Think of it as the remote control center for all your company's devices. From a web browser at portal.intune.microsoft.com, an admin can manage thousands of Windows laptops, iPhones, Android phones, and Macs — all from one place.

A Brief History
YearEvent
2011Microsoft launches Windows Intune as a subscription PC management service
2013Rebranded to Microsoft Intune; adds iOS and Android device management
2016Intune integrates with Azure Active Directory; major cloud expansion
2019Co-management with SCCM announced; Microsoft Endpoint Manager brand introduced
2022Microsoft Endpoint Manager split back; Intune gets its own dedicated portal
2023+Intune Suite launched — advanced features like Remote Help, Endpoint Privilege Management
What Exactly Can Intune Do?
💻
Device Management
Enroll, configure, monitor, and wipe Windows, macOS, iOS, Android devices. Push settings to thousands of devices at once.
📦
App Deployment
Push apps to devices silently. Deploy Microsoft 365 Apps, Win32 apps, store apps. Remove apps when needed.
🔐
Security Policies
Enforce BitLocker, require passwords, configure Defender Antivirus, set firewall rules — all from the cloud.
✅
Compliance
Mark devices as compliant or non-compliant. Block non-compliant devices from accessing company resources via Conditional Access.
📊
Reporting
See which devices are compliant, which apps failed to install, enrollment status, security posture — all in built-in reports.
🔄
Automation
Run PowerShell scripts, use Proactive Remediation to detect and fix issues automatically on devices without IT intervention.
The Intune Architecture

Intune is a SaaS (Software as a Service) product hosted entirely on Microsoft Azure. Here's how it works at a high level:

  1. 1
    Admin configures policies in the Intune portal (portal.intune.microsoft.com) — this is stored in Microsoft's cloud.
  2. 2
    Devices check in with Intune periodically (or on-demand). The MDM agent on the device contacts the Intune service.
  3. 3
    Policies are delivered to the device. Intune sends configuration, compliance rules, app assignments, scripts.
  4. 4
    Device reports back — compliance status, installed apps, last check-in time — all visible to admin in real-time.
  5. 5
    Conditional Access acts as a gatekeeper — only compliant, managed devices can access Microsoft 365 resources.
⚡ Why This Matters

Because Intune is cloud-based, it works for devices anywhere in the world — office, home, coffee shop, another country. Traditional on-premises tools like SCCM require VPN or direct network connectivity. Intune doesn't. This is why it became critical during COVID when everyone went remote.

🌍
Real-World Scenario

Situation: A 300-person company deploys new Windows 11 laptops to employees across 5 cities. Previously, IT had to either ship the laptop pre-imaged, or have someone on-site configure each machine.
With Intune: Laptop arrives at employee's home. Employee turns it on, connects to internet, logs in with company credentials. Windows Autopilot (powered by Intune) automatically enrolls the device, renames it, installs all required apps, applies security policies, joins Azure AD — all without IT touching the device. Employee is productive within 1–2 hours. IT never touched the machine physically.

🎤 Interview Questions — Topic 2
What is Microsoft Intune and what problem does it solve?
Intune is a cloud-based MDM and MAM solution from Microsoft. It solves the problem of managing and securing devices, apps, and corporate data across a distributed workforce — especially important for remote work scenarios where traditional on-prem tools fall short.
Is Intune on-premises or cloud-based?
Intune is entirely cloud-based, hosted on Microsoft Azure. There's no server to install or maintain. You access it through a web portal, and devices communicate with Microsoft's cloud over the internet.
What operating systems does Intune support?
Intune supports Windows 10/11, macOS, iOS/iPadOS, Android, and Android Enterprise. It also has limited support for Linux. It's truly cross-platform.
TOPIC 03
Intune vs SCCM vs Group Policy
Understanding When to Use Each Tool — and Why
Why There Are Three Tools

Microsoft didn't create these three tools to confuse you. Each was built for a different era and a different problem. Understanding their origins helps you understand why they exist and when to use which.

📜
Group Policy (GPO)
Born in 1999 with Windows 2000. Built for on-premises, domain-joined Windows PCs. It's the oldest and most mature — over 4,000 settings out of the box. But requires domain join and network connectivity.
🏗️
SCCM / MECM
Built in 2007 as an on-premises enterprise management suite. Extremely powerful for large enterprises — OS deployment, patching, software deployment, inventory. Requires significant infrastructure.
☁️
Microsoft Intune
Born for the cloud era (2011). No infrastructure, works anywhere, manages non-Windows devices too. The modern replacement — but not always a full replacement for SCCM in complex enterprises.
Detailed Comparison
Feature Group Policy SCCM Intune
Infrastructure neededDomain ControllerMultiple servers, SQLNone (cloud)
Device typesWindows onlyMostly WindowsWindows, Mac, iOS, Android
Works remotely❌ Needs VPN/domain⚠️ Needs VPN/CMG✅ Works anywhere
OS Deployment❌✅ Excellent⚠️ Autopilot only
App deployment⚠️ Limited✅ Very powerful✅ Good (Win32 support)
Patch management⚠️ Via WSUS only✅ Best in class✅ Windows Update for Business
Mobile devices❌❌✅ Native support
CostFree with Windows ServerHigh (licensing + infra)Included in M365/EMS
Settings granularity4,000+ settingsModerateGrowing (Settings Catalog)
Reporting❌ Very basic✅ Excellent (SSRS)✅ Good (built-in + Log Analytics)
Learning curveMediumVery HighMedium
The Decision Framework — Which to Use?
  • Pure cloud organization (new startup, no AD): Intune only. No need for GPO or SCCM.
  • Traditional enterprise, all on-prem: SCCM + GPO. Intune would add complexity without clear benefit.
  • Hybrid enterprise (most common): SCCM + Intune Co-management. Gradually move workloads to cloud.
  • Remote/global workforce: Intune essential. SCCM alone can't reach remote workers without Cloud Management Gateway (complex).
  • Multi-platform (Windows + Mac + Mobile): Intune is the only tool that manages all platforms natively.
  • Complex OS imaging needs: Keep SCCM. Intune's Autopilot doesn't replace traditional imaging for all scenarios.
✅ Industry Trend

The industry is clearly moving toward Intune. Microsoft's investment in SCCM has slowed — new features are coming to Intune. Most enterprises are on a journey: SCCM today → Co-management tomorrow → Intune-only eventually. As a new admin, mastering Intune is the right bet for your career.

🏦
Real-World Scenario

Company: A large bank with 10,000 employees.
Current state: Uses SCCM for everything — OS deployment, patching, software. Has 2,000 branch employees using old Windows 7 machines, now upgrading to Windows 11. Also now has 500 mobile workers using iPhones.
Decision: Keep SCCM for the 10,000 desktop machines (too complex to migrate fully). Add Intune for the 500 iPhone users (SCCM can't manage iOS). Enable Co-management so both tools work together. Eventually migrate compliance and update policies to Intune in the cloud so remote workers don't need VPN just to get patches.

🎤 Interview Questions — Topic 3
When would you choose Intune over SCCM?
I'd choose Intune when the environment has remote/mobile workers, BYOD requirements, non-Windows devices like iPhones or Macs, or when the organization wants to reduce on-premises infrastructure. Intune is also better for modern provisioning scenarios like Autopilot.
Can Group Policy settings be replicated in Intune?
Many GPO settings can be replicated using Intune's Settings Catalog and Administrative Templates (ADMX). Microsoft has a Group Policy Analytics tool in Intune that imports your existing GPOs and tells you which settings are supported in Intune. However, not all 4,000+ GPO settings have Intune equivalents yet.
What is Co-management?
Co-management is the ability to manage a Windows device with both SCCM and Intune simultaneously. You can split workloads — for example, keep software deployment in SCCM but move compliance policies and Windows Update to Intune. It's the bridge between on-prem and cloud management.
TOPIC 04
Microsoft 365 & Azure AD Relationship with Intune
Understanding the Microsoft Ecosystem — How Everything Connects
The Microsoft Cloud Ecosystem

Intune doesn't exist in isolation. It's one part of a much larger Microsoft cloud ecosystem. Understanding how the pieces connect is critical because in the real world, you'll configure things across multiple portals and services.

🔵
Azure Active Directory (Azure AD / Entra ID)
The identity backbone. Every user account, every device object, every group lives in Azure AD. Intune reads users and groups from Azure AD to target policies. Think of it as the directory that everyone else depends on.
🟢
Microsoft Intune
The device and app management layer. Uses Azure AD identity to know WHO a device belongs to and WHAT groups they're in — then delivers the right policies.
🟠
Microsoft 365 (M365)
The productivity suite — Exchange Online (email), SharePoint, Teams, OneDrive. Intune protects access to M365 resources through Compliance + Conditional Access integration.
How They Work Together — The Flow
  1. 1
    User exists in Azure AD. Created manually or synced from on-prem Active Directory via Azure AD Connect.
  2. 2
    User is assigned a license in Azure AD (e.g., Microsoft 365 Business Premium) — this license includes Intune.
  3. 3
    User enrolls their device in Intune. The device registers in Azure AD as an Azure AD Joined (or Hybrid joined) device.
  4. 4
    Intune assigns policies to the device based on the user's Azure AD group membership.
  5. 5
    Device reports compliance back to Intune. Azure AD reads this compliance status.
  6. 6
    Conditional Access policy in Azure AD checks: Is the user authenticated? Is the device compliant? If yes → allow access to Teams/Exchange/SharePoint. If no → block or prompt MFA.
💡 The Key Insight

Azure AD is the GLUE. Intune needs Azure AD for user identities and group targeting. M365 needs Azure AD for authentication. Conditional Access lives in Azure AD but reads compliance data from Intune. That's why every Intune environment requires Azure AD — you can't run Intune without it.

Azure AD Connect — Bridging On-Prem and Cloud

Most traditional enterprises have an on-premises Active Directory (AD DS) — the Windows Server domain controller that has existed for decades. Azure AD Connect is a tool that syncs user accounts from on-prem AD to Azure AD, allowing those users to access cloud services like Intune and M365.

ScenarioWhat's Used
New cloud-native company (no on-prem AD)Azure AD only. Users created directly in the cloud.
Traditional enterprise with on-prem ADAD on-prem + Azure AD Connect + Azure AD (hybrid). Users sync from on-prem to cloud.
Hybrid enterprise moving to cloudBoth AD on-prem and Azure AD. Devices can be Hybrid Azure AD Joined.
🏪
Real-World Scenario

Company: A retail chain with 2,000 employees.
Setup: Has on-prem Windows Server AD (used for 10 years). Now deploying M365 and Intune.
Solution: Install Azure AD Connect on their domain controller. This syncs all 2,000 employee accounts to Azure AD. Now those accounts can get M365 licenses assigned. Devices are Hybrid Azure AD Joined — they're still in the on-prem domain AND registered in Azure AD. Intune manages these devices. When an employee accesses SharePoint from their laptop, Conditional Access checks: is this device compliant in Intune? Is the user MFA-verified? If yes → access granted. The whole stack works together seamlessly.

🎤 Interview Questions — Topic 4
Can Intune work without Azure AD?
No. Azure AD is a prerequisite for Intune. Every device enrolled in Intune must have an Azure AD identity. Intune uses Azure AD groups to target policies, and Conditional Access (an Azure AD feature) enforces access based on Intune compliance data.
What is the relationship between Intune compliance and Conditional Access?
Intune evaluates devices against compliance policies and marks them compliant or non-compliant. Conditional Access in Azure AD uses that compliance status as a condition. If a device is non-compliant, Conditional Access can block access to Exchange, SharePoint, Teams — even if the user has valid credentials.
What is Azure AD Connect and why is it important for Intune?
Azure AD Connect syncs user accounts from on-premises Active Directory to Azure AD. This is important for Intune because organizations with existing on-prem AD need their users in Azure AD to be licensed and managed by Intune. Without sync, on-prem users wouldn't exist in the cloud.
TOPIC 05
Intune Licensing — P1, P2, E3, E5 Explained
Understanding Which License Gets You What Features
Why Licensing Matters for an Admin

As an L1/L2 admin, you won't buy licenses — but you WILL get tickets saying "this feature isn't working" and the root cause will often be the wrong or missing license. You need to know what's included in what license.

How Intune is Licensed

Intune is NOT a standalone product in most cases. It's included in several Microsoft subscription bundles. Here's the licensing landscape:

License Includes Intune? AAD P1? AAD P2? Defender? Best For
M365 Business Basic ❌❌❌❌ Small biz, email + Teams only
M365 Business Premium ✅✅❌✅ Basic SMB with security needs
M365 E3 ✅✅❌❌ Enterprise — productivity + compliance
M365 E5 ✅✅✅✅ Full Enterprise — maximum security
EMS E3 ✅✅❌❌ Add-on for enterprise management
EMS E5 ✅✅✅❌ Add-on with advanced identity
Intune Plan 1 (standalone) ✅ Core only❌❌❌ Pure device management add-on
Intune Plan 2 / Suite ✅ Advanced❌❌❌ Advanced features add-on
Azure AD P1 vs P2 — What Changes?
🔑
Azure AD P1 Gives You
Conditional Access policies, dynamic groups (auto-membership based on rules), self-service password reset, hybrid identity (Azure AD Connect), MFA. Required for most Intune enterprise features.
🛡️
Azure AD P2 Adds
Identity Protection (risk-based Conditional Access), Privileged Identity Management (PIM — just-in-time admin access), Access Reviews. Advanced security features for high-compliance environments.
Intune Suite — Advanced Add-ons

Microsoft introduced the Intune Suite as premium add-ons beyond Plan 1:

  • Remote Help: IT can remotely assist users directly through Intune portal. No third-party remote tools needed.
  • Endpoint Privilege Management (EPM): Allow standard users to run specific apps with admin rights — without giving them full admin access.
  • Advanced Endpoint Analytics: Deeper device performance and anomaly insights.
  • Microsoft Tunnel: VPN gateway for mobile devices to access on-prem resources.
  • Cloud PKI: Cloud-hosted certificate authority for Intune certificate deployment.
⚠️ Common Real-World Issue

You configure a Conditional Access policy requiring device compliance, but some users are being blocked even on managed devices. Root cause: their accounts don't have Azure AD P1 license assigned. Without P1, Conditional Access doesn't evaluate for that user. Always check license assignment when Conditional Access behaves unexpectedly.

How to Assign Intune Licenses
  1. 1
    Go to admin.microsoft.com → Users → Active Users
  2. 2
    Select the user → Click Licenses and apps
  3. 3
    Check the appropriate license (e.g., Microsoft 365 Business Premium)
  4. 4
    Save. License takes effect within minutes.
💡
Pro Tip: Use group-based licensing in Azure AD for large organizations. Assign the license to a group, and every member of that group automatically gets the license. As users join/leave the group, licenses are assigned/removed automatically. Far better than manual assignment for 500+ users.
🎤 Interview Questions — Topic 5
What license is required to use Microsoft Intune?
Intune is included in Microsoft 365 Business Premium, M365 E3, M365 E5, EMS E3, and EMS E5. It can also be purchased as a standalone Intune Plan 1 license. The specific features available depend on the license tier.
What features require Azure AD P2?
Azure AD P2 is needed for Identity Protection (risk-based Conditional Access), Privileged Identity Management (PIM for just-in-time admin roles), and Access Reviews. Basic Conditional Access only needs P1.
A user says they can't enroll their device in Intune. What's the first thing you check?
I'd first check if the user has an Intune license assigned. Go to admin.microsoft.com or Azure AD, find the user, check their assigned licenses. Without an Intune license, device enrollment will fail with a "license not found" type error.

What's the difference between Endpoint Security policies and Configuration Profiles?

TOPIC 07
Tenant Setup — DNS, Domain, Admin Roles
Setting Up Your Microsoft 365 Tenant for Intune
What is a Tenant?

A tenant is your organization's dedicated instance of Microsoft's cloud services. When your company subscribes to Microsoft 365, Microsoft creates a tenant — an isolated environment with your users, devices, data, and configurations. No other company can see or access your tenant.

Every tenant gets a default domain: yourcompanyname.onmicrosoft.com. But you'll want to add your real company domain (e.g., contoso.com) so users sign in with their real email addresses.

Step-by-Step Tenant Setup for Intune
  1. 1
    Purchase M365 Subscription — through Microsoft directly, a partner, or CSP. This creates your tenant. You'll get a Global Admin account with yourname@tenantname.onmicrosoft.com.
  2. 2
    Add Custom Domain — Go to admin.microsoft.com → Settings → Domains → Add domain. Enter your company domain (e.g., contoso.com).
  3. 3
    Verify Domain via DNS — Microsoft gives you a TXT record to add to your domain's DNS (at GoDaddy, Cloudflare, etc.). This proves you own the domain. Microsoft then verifies it automatically.
  4. 4
    Add MX and other DNS Records — For Exchange Online email to work, add MX, CNAME, and SPF records that Microsoft provides. Users can then receive email at name@contoso.com.
  5. 5
    Assign Licenses — Assign M365 licenses to users so they get access to Intune, Exchange, etc.
  6. 6
    Configure Intune — Go to portal.intune.microsoft.com. Set MDM Authority to Intune (this was a manual step in older tenants; now auto-configured). Set up enrollment restrictions, company branding, etc.
  7. 7
    Set MDM Scope — In Azure AD → Mobility (MDM and MAM) → Microsoft Intune — set the MDM User scope to All or a specific group. This controls which users can enroll devices.

📋 Intune CNAME Records for Auto-Enrollment

For Windows devices to auto-detect and enroll in Intune when a user enters their email during OOBE (Out-of-Box Experience), you need to create CNAME DNS records:
enterpriseenrollment.yourdomain.com → enterpriseenrollment.manage.microsoft.com
enterpriseregistration.yourdomain.com → enterpriseregistration.windows.net
Without these, users need to manually enter the Intune server URL during enrollment — not a great experience.

MDM Authority — Critical Setting

The MDM Authority tells the tenant which MDM solution is in charge. Options are Intune or SCCM (for co-management). You cannot easily switch this after devices are enrolled, so getting it right from day one matters.

  • Intune MDM Authority: For Intune-only environments.
  • SCCM MDM Authority: For co-management (SCCM manages, Intune assists).
  • New tenants: Auto-set to Intune. No action needed.
🔧
Real-World Scenario

Problem: Users at a newly onboarded company complain that when they try to enroll their Windows PCs, they have to manually type a long server URL. Other users can't figure out how to do it and give up.
Root cause: Missing EnterpriseEnrollment CNAME DNS record. Without it, Windows can't auto-discover the Intune enrollment server.
Fix: Go to your domain's DNS registrar → add CNAME record → enterpriseenrollment → enterpriseenrollment.manage.microsoft.com → wait 24-48hrs for DNS propagation. Enrollment is now seamless — users just enter their email, Windows does the rest.

🎤 Interview Questions — Topic 7
What DNS records are needed for Intune to work properly?
For domain verification: a TXT record. For email: MX and CNAME autodiscover records. For seamless Windows enrollment: CNAME records for enterpriseenrollment and enterpriseregistration pointing to Microsoft's enrollment service. For email security: SPF TXT record.
What is MDM Authority and why does it matter?
MDM Authority determines which management platform is in charge of device management — Intune or SCCM. It's a critical tenant-level setting that can't easily be changed after devices are enrolled. For Intune-only environments, it should be set to Intune. For co-management, it's more nuanced.
TOPIC 08
Azure AD Roles vs Intune Roles
Understanding Access Control — Who Can Do What
Why Two Role Systems?

Intune runs on top of Azure AD. Azure AD has its own role system (RBAC) for managing the Azure AD service and tenant. Intune has a separate RBAC system for managing device and app management tasks. They're different systems that overlap in some areas — which confuses many new admins.

Intune Administrator Full Intune access. Cannot manage Azure AD itself (users, groups, Conditional Access). Your dedicated Intune admin. Best fit for most endpoint admins.
Conditional Access Administrator Create/modify Conditional Access policies in Azure AD. Works with Intune compliance data. Security team member who manages access control policies.
Security Reader Read-only view of Intune security data, Defender data, compliance reports. SOC analysts who need visibility without making changes.

Within Intune, there's a separate RBAC system. These roles control what someone can do inside Intune — regardless of their Azure AD role (except Global Admin and Intune Admin, which bypass Intune RBAC).

Scope Tags — Restricting by Geography or Group

Scope Tags are one of the most important and underutilized Intune RBAC features. They let you limit what an admin can SEE and MANAGE to a specific subset of devices/users.

🌏
Real-World Example of Scope Tags

Company: Global organization with offices in India, USA, and UK.
Problem: The India IT team should only manage India devices. The USA team should only manage USA devices. But everyone shares the same Intune tenant.
Solution: Create scope tags: "India", "USA", "UK". Tag all India devices with the "India" scope tag. Assign the India IT team the Intune Policy and Profile Manager role scoped to the "India" scope tag. Now when the India admin logs into Intune, they only see India devices. They can't accidentally wipe or modify a USA device.
Result: Delegated administration with data isolation — without creating separate tenants.

✅ Best Practice

Follow the Principle of Least Privilege. Don't give everyone Global Admin. L1 helpdesk gets Intune Help Desk Operator. L2 gets Policy and Profile Manager. Senior admin gets Intune Administrator (Azure AD role). Create custom Intune roles for teams with specific needs. Use scope tags to prevent cross-regional accidents. This is what interviewers want to hear when they ask about access control.

🎤 Interview Questions — Topic 8
What's the difference between the Global Administrator role and the Intune Administrator role?
Global Admin has unrestricted access to everything in the tenant — Azure AD, M365, billing, Intune. Intune Administrator has full Intune access but cannot manage Azure AD itself (users, groups, Conditional Access). For daily Intune administration, Intune Administrator is the appropriate role — Global Admin is too powerful and a security risk for routine use.
What are Scope Tags in Intune and when would you use them?
Scope Tags restrict what an Intune admin can see and manage to a specific subset of devices and policies. I'd use them in multi-region or multi-department environments to ensure each IT team can only manage their own devices — preventing accidental changes to other regions' configurations.
Can you create custom roles in Intune?
Yes. Intune allows creating custom roles with specific permissions tailored to your organization's needs. For example, you could create a "App Deployment Only" role that lets someone push apps but not touch device configuration policies. Custom roles are scoped using Scope Tags.
What role would you give a helpdesk technician who needs to wipe lost devices?
I'd give them the Intune Help Desk Operator role. This allows them to view device information and perform remote actions like wipe, restart, and sync — but they can't create or modify policies. Combined with appropriate Scope Tags to limit them to their region's devices.
